Project Idea

Overview

Weather is still not entirely predictable, but there are newly emerging technologies that can help us gain a better understanding. 5G and IoT devices open an entirely new realm of possibilities for how we collect weather data, and how it is processed. Utilising low powered connected devices, the sheer amount of data that could be collected would alter how we model weather entirely. This would allow new insights into microclimates and a deeper understanding of how each system affects others. A more granular system of collection ensures higher accuracy and more advance modelling and forecasting for localised areas.

Motivation

In Australia, the forecasts and observations are collected and determined using a network of weather stations. (Burea of Meterorlogy, 2018) This model provides an understanding of the macro effects of weather over the country but can neglect the localised factors that play a part in the system. Trees, buildings, hills and other structures influence the observations recorded. (Bureau of Meteorology, n.d) Increasing the number of sites from which data is collected would allow for a deeper understanding of this phenomenon and a more accurate model. Advances in connectivity through technologies like 5G, and the increasing availability of low cost connected devices allow a change in the way we collect and utilise weather data.

Description

The bulk of weather data collected in Australia consists of air pressure, temperature, humidity, wind speed and direction, and rainfall. This data is used to model weather and provide forecasts. The method in which this data is modelled uses large grids. ‘The size of the grids is limited by the data density across Australia’ (Bureau of Meteorology, 2009) With the uprising of 5G technologies, combined with the increasing range of IoT devices a solution can be developed that results in more granular data. The aim is to reduce the size of these grids and gain an understanding of the microclimates of local areas.

Increasing the number of sensors utilised could be achieved in several ways. Opening the opportunity for consumers to participate in the collection would be an ideal solution for increasing accuracy in suburban areas. It could also be done on a private basis, or in conjunction with specific companies. The agriculture sector for instance, would benefit greatly from the ability to see a real time model of the conditions affecting their crops. Both natural and artificial structures such as hills, building and trees affect the microclimate (MetLink, n.d), and these insights could be invaluable to better implementing informed actions in industries where climate is a factor. A combination of these two options would be the ideal approach as it would allow increased uptake, and thus, more data for modelling.

A similar model of this would be that of Flightradar24. They offer the ability for regular consumers to assist in mapping flights, and then process user collected data, in conjunction with both privately collected data and official data. (Flightradar24, n.d) There are several home weather devices on the market already today, however accuracy is important. Ensuring the sensors used meet the requirements of WMO, World Meteorological Organisation, will mean the results are highly accurate. The option of allowing consumers to build their own weather station using Raspberry Pi or Arduino could also be explored, providing there are clear guidelines. The sensors are currently still quite expensive, which may be a drawback in initial user uptake, and corporate integration.

For connectivity, Wi-Fi or low energy Bluetooth will need to be installed in each sensor system. The exact technologies used form uploading the data would likely vary, dependant on their implemented technology locally. Technology like 5G will allow seamless connectivity to a huge number of sensors through the ability to offer low power connected solutions. (Qualcomm, n.d) In more rural areas however, cable or satellite options may need to be implemented. The project would likely be implemented mostly in places where internet connectivity will already be in place and existing technology can be used.

The collected data would be received in real time, or short intervals, to ensure the model is constantly updated. The back end of the software would utilize powerful cloud-based processing and require a complex metrological algorithm and data base to be built into it. A front-end system for viewing the processed data would need to be created showcasing current observations and forecasts.

Tools and technologies

The major focus for this project would be building a platform that can collect and collate the data in real time, from the vast array of sensors in place. Existing technology for collecting weather data can be utilised as well as building new connected devices using Raspberry Pi or Arduino and high-quality sensors. Utilising official data from entities such as BoM, NOAA and ECMWF, will allow the data collected to be crosschecked, and increase the scale of the model. A powerful processing algorithm and database will need to be created to provide will provide both up to date observations of the weather, but also forecasts in real time.

Skills required

An understanding of weather modelling, or access to resources offering an advanced understanding will be needed. Cloud services will also have to be utilised to process the data in a centralised location. A comprehensive weather modelling algorithm will need to be created and implemented into this cloud system. A frontend system will also be required to be developed to allow uploading of weather data, likely a website and installed app. There are plenty of developers with this skillset, however converting meteorological concepts to an algorithm may limit the options for back-end. Hardware is also readily available but may be currently cost prohibitive to some people or industries.

Outcome

If this project was successful, the outcome would be a deeper understanding of how microclimates affect the macro climate. This insight could prove valuable to several climate reliant industries such as farming. Beyond that the insights could be utilised by plenty of fields of science to further examine how climate and weather play a role in their field of study. Everyday consumers will have access to accurate localised weather observations and forecasting that will more closely resemble their real conditions.
